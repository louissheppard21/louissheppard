Biffa. Transforming the end-to-end customer experience, taking the business from flatlined sales to a 61% increase and driving an organisational shift to online self-serve.

Context

  • Organisation - Biffa, UK waste utility provider
  • Role - Experience Strategy & Design Lead
  • Scope - Multi-year transformation across sales and customer service

Problem

  • Phone-based sales & service limiting scalability
  • Fragmented operational systems
  • Complex regulatory service configuration
  • Poor service visibility for customers

Impact

  • 61% YoY SME growth
  • 38% reduction in support calls
  • 5 days → 15 minute onboarding
  • Shift to scalable self-serve

Introduction

Biffa, a major UK waste utility provider, was undertaking a multi-year transformation to embed digital self-service across its SME and enterprise customer base. The aim was to drive revenue growth, improve customer satisfaction and reduce reliance on manual service operations.

I led experience strategy and design across the programme, ensuring delivery remained focused on research-driven customer outcomes aligned with commercial and operational goals.

The Problem

Flatlined sales and future scalability limited by fragmented, manual process.

Biffa’s growth relied heavily on phone-based sales supported by fragmented internal systems and manual operational processes. This created slow onboarding times, poor service visibility for customers and rising operational costs. Digital channels existed but were unable to support the operational complexity of Biffa’s services.

The challenge was to design self-serve journeys capable of supporting highly complex service configurations while remaining simple and trustworthy enough customers to adopt with confidence, without introducing unacceptable operational or regulatory risk.

My Role

I lead experience strategy and design across the program, responsible for:

  • Leading and coaching designers across multiple workstreams
  • Early ideation, prototyping, user flows and interaction design for key product features
  • Planning and running customer research to inform product strategy
  • Setting the experience vision and maintaining long-term product direction
  • Facilitating cross-functional workshops to generate and refine solution concepts
  • Using service design to connect digital interfaces with operational systems and offline touchpoints
  • Partnering closely with product and engineering to define success metrics, align priorities and negotiate trade-offs
  • Aligning design teams and senior stakeholders across a complex organisational environment
  • Overseeing design system development and cross-team adoption

Research & Strategy

Roadmap priorities driven by evidenced insight and aligned to clear success measures.

I ran a research programme combining SME and enterprise customer interviews, surveys, service desk call listening, analysis of existing digital journeys and stakeholder interviews.

While customer needs varied, consistent patterns emerged. Customers expected waste services to operate in the background of their business. The relationship with Biffa only became visible when something went wrong. Poor communication and limited service visibility amplified frustration and focused attention on these negative moments.

Research also revealed widespread misconceptions about waste processing and environmental impact, particularly among SME customers. Incorrect service configuration during onboarding was common and often resulted in unexpected charges and contract changes, damaging trust early in the relationship.

These insights highlighted an opportunity to shift the experience away from reactive issue management toward clearer communication, proactive visibility and stronger customer confidence.

Working with product leadership, I translated customer insights into roadmap priorities and established a KPI framework to guide product development decisions. I also developed a product vision and experience strategy, presenting this to executive stakeholders to secure sponsorship.

Key Product Decisions

Phased self-serve rollout.

To reduce operational and regulatory risk while demonstrating early commercial value, I proposed a phased delivery approach:

  • Phase 1: SME eCommerce onboarding
  • Phase 2: Core self-serve account management
  • Phase 3: Enterprise tooling and complex service changes

This allowed the organisation to validate digital adoption with SME customers before expanding into more complex areas.

Adaptive experience framework.

The platform needed to support customers ranging from single-site SMEs to enterprise organisations managing large service estates. I oversaw the creation of a modular design system and interaction framework that allowed interfaces to adapt to the scale and complexity of customer data.

Designing for imperfect operational data.

Customers wanted better visibility of their services, but gaps and latency in operational data made full transparency difficult. I advocated for an approach that always provided status updates or next actions even when complete information was unavailable. Messaging, notifications and service statuses were mapped to available operational signals to bridge these gaps.

Environmental insight as a growth lever.

Customer research revealed strong interest in environmental impact reporting. We used this insight to support both customer value and commercial growth. Detailed reporting tools supported enterprise needs, while simplified insights helped SME customers understand the carbon impact of their services and encouraged adoption of additional waste services.

Design Approach

Service design-led thinking with continous testing and iteration.

I prioritised service design ahead of detailed interface work, using cross-functional workshops to model the full service journey including backstage processes and data dependencies. This ensured the experience was grounded in operational reality and allowed engineering teams to identify upstream system changes early.

From this foundation we developed lightweight prototypes for key journeys, validating them with engineering and testing with customers. These prototypes helped communicate direction and feasibility to the wider organisation while assessing commercial viability and appetite for risk.

Throughout delivery I supported my team by unblocking challenges and contributing hands-on journey and interaction design for key features. Regular design clinics helped the team review problems, align approaches and maintain consistency with strategic goals.

Following each phase launch we adopted a hypothesis-driven experimentation approach, using behavioural data and customer feedback to prioritise improvements. This allowed the team to continuously optimise onboarding, pricing clarity and service configuration while balancing delivery of new capabilities.

Outcomes

  • Took flatlined SME sales to 61% YoY increase, significantly exceeding targets
  • 38% reduction in service desk calls, lowering operational costs
  • Faster service contracting time from 5 days to under 15 minutes with fewer manual processing errors.
  • A measurable cultural shift toward customer-centred decision making, driven by visible commercial impact
  • Established an embedded test-and-learn optimisation capability to drive ongoing product improvement

The Open. A digital ecosystem for the iconic golf championship to drive monetization and fandom, increasing digital revenue by 78% and selling out the event for the first time in it’s 150 year history.

Context

  • Organisation - The R&A, organisers of The Open Championship
  • Role - Experience Strategy & Design Lead
  • Scope - Digital ecosystem across marketing, ticketing, year-round engagement and live event coverage

Problem

  • Challenge growing audience share
  • Highly competitive global market
  • Lack of growth in ticket sales and online revenue
  • Complex live event dynamics requiring continuous updates

Impact

  • Sold out The Open for the first time in 150 year
  • 76% increase in attributable digital revenue
  • 250k new memberships generated during the first event
  • 56% increase in combined engagement metrics

Introduction

As competition between global sports brands intensified, The Open sought to grow its audience and strengthen its commercial performance in order to secure its position as one of the world’s leading sporting events.

I led experience strategy and design for a multi-year programme to create a new fan engagement ecosystem spanning marketing, ticket sales, year-round engagement and live championship coverage across web and mobile.

The Problem

Lack of commercial growth and highly competitive market for new fan aquisition.

The Open’s digital platforms primarily served an existing audience of dedicated golf fans. However, research indicated that this audience alone would not sustain long-term growth, particularly as other sports brands invested heavily in global fan engagement.

Market research also revealed a perceived barrier for potential new audiences, with golf often seen as difficult to understand or inaccessible. At the same time, monetising existing audiences was becoming more challenging as competing platforms provided coverage of the event.

The R&A had established a five-year strategic objective to sell out The Open for the first time in its history while significantly expanding its global audience and commercial performance.

The challenge was to design a digital ecosystem capable of attracting new audiences, increasing engagement and driving ticket sales while operating within immovable tournament and ticket release deadlines.

My Role

I lead design and experience strategy across the programme, responsible for:

  • Defining the experience strategy and product design principles
  • Facilitating opportunity framing and co-creation workshops with cross-functional teams and business stakeholders
  • Leading design direction across ticketing, year-round engagement and live event experiences across web and app
  • Hands-on design of key journeys, interaction patterns and interface concepts
  • Partnering with Product to define success measures across each phase
  • Developing the visual system and evolving the brand for digital channels
  • Establishing a scalable design system
  • Planning and running user testing to validate key journeys
  • Supporting prioritisation and sequencing against immovable tournament deadlines

Research & Strategy

Operational immersion and data-led research.

The R&A had invested in extensive market research and developed audience segmentation and personas. These provided a foundation for understanding the motivations and behaviours of different fan groups.

To deepen this understanding, my team and I attended The Open and embedded within operational teams to observe how the championship was delivered.

This included:

  • Working alongside journalism and content teams within the media centre
  • Course walkthroughs with scoring and data teams
  • Meeting with media and sponsorship partners
  • Speaking directly with attending fans

I also analysed engagement data across the year-round build-up to the championship, identifying patterns in how fans interacted with content during qualification stages, ticket releases and the live event.

Using these insights, I facilitated workshops with cross-functional leaders and stakeholders to map key fan journeys and identify critical moments across the season where engagement and commercial impact could be strengthened.

Key Product Decisions

Reframing the ticketing experience.

The existing ticket purchase journey experienced high abandonment rates. We reframed ticket sales as part of the broader championship narrative, using archive content and tournament storytelling to build anticipation and emotional connection.

A ticket builder journey was designed to guide fans through staggered decisions, reducing cognitive load and improving completion rates.

Layered engagement model.

During the live championship, engagement directly influenced commercial value, with advertising impressions, retail purchases and membership sign-ups tied closely to user activity.

I developed a layered content model consisting of an Interest layer with fast, digestible updates and highlights, and an Insight layer for deeper analysis and detailed content for engaged fans.

Automated live event experience.

Working with engineering, I designed automation logic based on key on-course events that dynamically adapted the experience and surfaced relevant content.

This reduced the need for constant manual updates by editorial teams while ensuring the platform remained responsive to live play.

The system also enabled improved targeting of advertising and promotional placements, helping the organisation meet sponsor commitments while increasing visibility for owned promotions.

Improving content discovery.

Analytics showed that most users concentrated on the leaderboard while large volumes of editorial and video content went undiscovered.

I introduced interaction principles that allowed fans to access related stories, highlights and statistics from any point within the experience, encouraging deeper exploration of the platform.

Tools for editorial teams.

To support broader storytelling during the championship, I designed a custom publishing interface that enabled content teams to rapidly create short-form stories and updates.

This allowed journalists to respond to developing narratives across the tournament and significantly increase the volume of content available to fans.

Design Approach

Co-creation to explore solutions and testing to de-risk delivery.

Delivery was structured around three phases aligned to immovable championship deadlines. Ahead of each phase I ran co-design workshops with product, engineering and commercial stakeholders to define problems and generate solution concepts.

Early ideas were rapidly developed into interactive prototypes that we tested with fans and used to align stakeholders before detailed interface design began.

User testing continued throughout the design process, helping refine interactions and language while reducing delivery risk ahead of critical ticket release windows and the live event.

Alongside the product experience, I developed a visual direction that evolved The Open’s brand for digital channels. The goal was to retain its heritage while presenting a more modern and accessible identity.

This helped position the championship as both iconic and contemporary, challenging perceptions that golf was difficult to engage with.

Outcomes

  • Sold out The Open for the first time in its 150-year history
  • Achieved this five years ahead of the organisation’s strategic target
  • 76% increase in attributable digital revenue during the championship
  • 250,000 new membership sign-ups during the first event
  • 56% increase in engagement metrics across the digital ecosystem
  • Introduced waiting lists and early access mechanisms for subsequent championships

First Bus. Designing the future of travel for millions of customers, guiding a major, multi-year program to drive new revenue and build long-term loyalty.

Context

  • Organisation - First Bus, one of the UK’s largest public transport operators
  • Role - Experience Strategy & Design Lead
  • Scope - Customer experience strategy across digital products, offline touchpoints and operational systems

Problem

  • Regionalised operating model fragmenting customer experience
  • Limited ability to capture data and build intelligence
  • Lack of growth through regional marketing approach
  • Ambition to drive revenue and loyalty without executable plan

Impact

  • Directly informed a multi-million-pound investment
  • Created a customer ecosystem blueprint
  • Aligned regional and central leadership around a vision
  • Set actionable plan for delivery

Introduction

First Bus was planning a strategic shift from a business primarily focused on operational efficiency toward one centred on revenue growth and customer experience. The organisation was preparing to invest heavily in technology and data but lacked a coherent vision for how digital products, offline touchpoints and operational change would work together to deliver its commercial ambitions.

I led experience and design strategy within a cross-functional team to define the long-term customer experience vision, shape the product roadmap and support the business case for transformation.

The Problem

Regionalised approach lacking a centralised customer experience strategy.

First Bus operated through a highly regionalised structure with limited governance over the end-to-end customer experience. As a result, customer journeys varied significantly between regions and channels.

Digital products, ticketing systems and physical touchpoints such as buses and stations were poorly connected. This fragmentation created confusion for customers while limiting the organisation’s ability to capture meaningful data about travel behaviour.

Without a unified view of the customer journey, the business struggled to identify new revenue opportunities or build momentum internally for transformation. The challenge was to define a credible, evidence-led vision for the future customer experience that could secure executive approval, align regional stakeholders and translate strategic ambition into an actionable delivery roadmap.

My Role

I led experience strategy and design concept development, responsible for:

  • Leading discovery and research to inform the strategic direction
  • Facilitating alignment across product, engineering, data and operations
  • Designing future-state journeys and ecosystem concepts
  • Defining the role of digital products within the wider service experience
  • Supporting the business case for transformation at executive level

Research & Strategy

Designing a future-state customer journey supported by actionable strategic assets.

I led research to understand the existing customer experience and operational constraints across the organisation. This involved working closely with regional leadership, marketing teams and customer service teams while reviewing existing customer feedback and operational data.

Through a series of workshops I mapped the current-state journey and collaborated with product, data and engineering leads to identify integration gaps across the ecosystem.This work exposed several structural challenges:

  • Major inconsistencies between web, app and physical touchpoints
  • Confusing ticketing and pricing models across regions
  • Limited ability to capture and use customer data
  • Few opportunities to deliver personalised services or build loyalty

This model showed how digital products, operational systems and physical touchpoints could work together to support the revenue and loyalty goals the organisation had set.

Working with cross-functional leads, we then defined the supporting capabilities required at each stage of the journey, including technology infrastructure, customer data systems and content operations. This work resulted in a set of strategic artefacts that would guide transformation delivery, including:

  • A customer ecosystem blueprint
  • Content and information architecture
  • Reporting and measurement framework
  • Customer data strategy
  • Target operating model
  • Product roadmap

Key Strategic Decisions

Designing the experience as an ecosystem.

Rather than focusing solely on digital products, we framed the opportunity as a connected service ecosystem linking apps, web platforms, physical journeys and operational systems. This helped the organisation understand that meaningful improvement required coordinated change across technology, operations and customer communications.

Using customer data as the foundation for growth.

The research highlighted that First Bus had limited ability to understand or personalise the customer experience. We defined a customer data strategy that would allow the organisation to capture meaningful behavioural insight and use this to drive loyalty programmes, targeted offers and improved journey planning.

Aligning transformation around clear customer journeys.

Future-state journey models were used to align leadership teams around a shared understanding of the customer experience and the opportunities for growth. This helped move discussions away from technology implementation toward customer outcomes and commercial impact.

Design Approach

Design concepts to align teams and secure executive buy-in.

To make the transformation vision tangible for leadership teams, I created high-fidelity prototypes and visual storyboards that illustrated how the future customer experience would work in practice. These prototypes demonstrated how integrated digital products, customer data and operational improvements could create a more coherent travel experience while unlocking new revenue opportunities.

I also facilitated co-creation workshops with product, engineering and design teams around key moments in the journey. These sessions generated solution concepts and helped validate technical feasibility ahead of further planning.

The resulting concepts were shared across the organisation and played an important role in building alignment between regional operators and central leadership teams.

Outcomes & Impact

  • Directly informed a multi-million-pound transformation investment now in delivery
  • Established a clear blueprint for digital product delivery and operational change
  • Aligned regional and central teams around a shared customer vision
  • Identified new revenue growth opportunities tied to loyalty and personalisation
  • Helped to shift leadership mindset from operational efficiency to experience-led growth

Speedy Hire. eCommerce and service transformation across a product ecosystem, re-designing business processes, foundational systems and customer experiences.

Context

  • Organisation - Speedy Hire, UK’s largest equipment hire provider
  • Role - Experience Strategy & Design Lead
  • Scope - eCommerce and service transformation across web, mobile app and point-of-sale system

Problem

  • High abandonment across all digital journeys
  • Deep rooted foundational issues
  • Lack of capability to meet customer expectation
  • Platform rebuild underway without a customer vision

Impact

  • Prevented critical systems issues being locked in
  • Redesigned product classification model
  • Established scalable design system
  • Aligned product and engineering to customer vision
  • Redesigned business processes and rebalanced risk approach

Introduction

Speedy Hire, the UK’s largest equipment hire provider, was undertaking a multi-year programme to transform its digital commerce ecosystem across web, mobile and point-of-sale systems.

The transformation was supported by major investment in logistics, data infrastructure and operational change. However, early delivery lacked a coherent product vision and risked embedding structural issues from the legacy platform into the new system.

I led experience strategy and design across the programme, ensuring delivery remained focused on customer outcomes rather than short-term technical constraints.

The Problem

High abandonment across all journeys and digital infrastructure work underway without customer vision alignment.

The existing eCommerce platform was built on legacy systems with a deeply flawed product taxonomy and fragmented user journeys.

Search and navigation issues were widespread and significantly impacted customers’ ability to find and hire equipment. These problems were not purely interface issues but rooted in underlying catalogue structure and business rules.

At the same time, product and engineering teams were progressing platform rebuild work without a shared understanding of how their efforts would combine to deliver meaningful customer outcomes.

Without intervention, there was a significant risk that the new infrastructure would replicate the same structural problems as the legacy platform.

My Role

I led experience strategy and design across the programme, responsible for:

  • Leading and coaching a design team across development of the web, app and point-of-sale ecosystem
  • Mapping current journeys, operational processes and pain points through service design
  • Advocating for structural fixes rather than superficial interface improvements
  • Collaborating with product and engineering teams on roadmap planning and delivery sequencing
  • Leading research to inform product strategy and validate concepts
  • Redesigning the product catalogue taxonomy using keyword and sales data, AI modelling and tree testing
  • Facilitating workshops and prototyping to highlight underlying business process issues
  • Establishing and governing a scalable design system

Research & Strategy

Identifying critical systems failures impacting experience and conversion.

Research included customer journey workshops, user testing of the existing digital experience, analysis of search and sales data, and consultation with business stakeholders.

This work revealed that the existing product taxonomy was fundamentally misaligned with customer intent. Search and navigation failures were therefore structural rather than cosmetic. With platform development already underway, there was a critical risk that these problems would be permanently embedded in the new infrastructure.

Previous mergers and acquisitions had also created a fragmented service offering. The organisation was investing in brand development to clarify its value proposition, but this required a fundamental rethink of how the service was structured digitally.

To address this, I developed a product vision and set of experience principles to guide delivery. I also created a future-state journey model that aligned product teams around how individual workstreams would contribute to customer outcomes.

Working with product leadership, I established a KPI framework that connected design decisions to wider organisational objectives.

Key Product Decisions

Rebuilding the product taxonomy.

Early platform work assumed the existing catalogue structure would be retained. I presented evidence from research and analytics showing that the taxonomy was misaligned with customer intent and would undermine the success of the new platform.

Despite delivery pressure, I advocated for a full redesign of the classification model supported by keyword analysis, AI modelling and tree testing. This required trading short-term delivery speed for long-term product viability.

Reframing business rules within the purchase journey.

Purchase and account creation journeys were heavily constrained by rules around fraud prevention, deposits, late charges and returns. These rules were poorly communicated and created significant friction.

I modelled user flows to highlight where these rules were blocking progress and used prototypes to explore alternative approaches. This enabled productive discussions with business leaders and resulted in changes to several underlying policies.

Supporting new customer segments.

Alongside serving its core trade customers, Speedy Hire wanted to grow within the home DIY market.

Research revealed that these customers struggled to match equipment to their projects. We introduced new categorisation and tagging models that helped customers identify appropriate tools based on project type and skill level.

Design Approach

Strong design system foundations to scale across multiple workstreams and phases.

Once a future-state customer journey model was established, design work was planned around complete functional areas rather than individual features.

As all digital products were being rebuilt alongside a brand refresh, this also provided an opportunity to establish a new design system.

I developed conceptual interface designs for key pages to explore the visual direction and align brand expression with experience goals. From this foundation we created a shared set of components, principles and design foundations.

Regular cross-functional design reviews helped teams agree how the system should evolve while maintaining consistency. Over time this developed into a governance model for ongoing contributions and maintenance.

In parallel, I worked with product leadership to define a KPI framework linking experience improvements to organisational goals. Design work was tracked against these metrics to ensure visibility of how experience improvements contributed to strategic outcomes.

Outcomes & Impact

    The programme is ongoing, with first major launch scheduled for FY2026 however it has already delivered tangible value.

  • Prevented flawed taxonomy from being embedded in new platform infrastructure
  • Improved product discoverability and reduced search failure in early testing
  • Established a scalable design system adopted across teams
  • Introduced clearer customer account structures
  • Enabled changes to critical business rules while maintaining acceptable risk levels
  • Created a journey-led roadmap guiding future delivery
